mello, a mental health support app

We created a mental health app incorporating both immediate and longer-term support to help further the mission of the National Suicide Prevention Lifeline

Researching


background





The Centers for Disease Control and
Prevention
Sadly, there are a ton of data out there that support why suicide continues to be a public health issue, and why it is critical to care for mental health.

We wanted to look more into how we could hypothetically work with the National Suicide Prevention Lifeline. They're a leader in crisis services and advancing suicide prevention, and we were wondering if we could develop a mobile app to supplement their current website.






user research plan
For more insight into our users' relationship with their mental health and how they cope with times of crises, our main research objectives were to understand how to support users during and between times of suicidal crisis and emotional distress as well as explore how users handle times of distress.

We asked heavy questions such as:
“How would you describe the current state of your mental health?”

“Could you talk about what you do to relieve stress and anxiety?”

“When was the last time you had suicidal thoughts or were in extreme emotional distress? If you're comfortable, would you mind describing the experience?”

“If it's okay with you, we'd like to hear more about how you feel discussing suicidal thoughts with your friends or family.”






results
Key recurring themes were importance of a strong support system, lack of motivation to seek help, lack of mental health resources, fear of or reluctance to discussing mental health with loved ones, and pressure of other external life stressors



From our 13 Google survey results, we found that 61% would reach out to their family during a time of crisis or mental distress.

Additionally, the majority of users resorted to exercise, music, and yoga/meditation to cope with emotional distress.








user persona






problem statement
We were beginning to see that continuity of emotional and mental care was very important to our users.

How might we provide continuous mental health support to users during and after times of suicidal crisis and emotional distress?






summary
We wanted to focus this project in the mental health space and figure out how we could develop a mobile app to support the National Suicide Prevention Lifeline. After conducting user research and synthesizing the results, we were realizing the importance of an app that is there for users as much as possible, both during and outside times of suicidal and emotional crisis.

Ideating


brainstorming




We were thinking about features that would altogether act as a support system for our users. This meant an app that could check in with their headspace and serve as a reliable resource in times of immediate need.

After conducting competitor analyses (not shown), we saw that not many apps out there offered both short-term and long-term mental health support, understandably because of the breadth of what this would entail. We wanted to try and see if we could settle in this niche without reaching too far beyond our scope.






user flow


So what would our user exactly experience? After storyboarding a potential user journey and individually concepting user flows, our group landed on a user flow that emphasized the current Lifeline support feature as well as access to mental health resources involving self-care.






summary
Continuing to refer back to our research and looking around at what was already out there, we were better understanding what our app could offer users. We wanted to create an app that could act as an immediate and long-term support system, which would include the Lifeline for users to talk to someone and various mental health resources for the day-to-day experience.

Prototyping and Testing


sketches


Drawing from our research results, we focused our mental health resources on guided meditation, exercises, music, and checking in on users' mood.






lo-fi prototype

     

     






usability testing
Each team member then ran our app through different people. Our goals were to see if users found our main features–connecting with Lifeline and mental health care resources–helpful and intuitive. We wanted to make sure that the app actually supports the user.

In addition to testing our features, we asked questions such as:
  • How do you feel about the app overall? Would you find it personally supportive of your mental health?

  • Do you have suggestions on what we can add or remove?

  • Could you describe how you feel about the overall tone of our app?
Based on these results, we created an onboarding section, enhanced personalization by adding suggested resources based on in-app activity, and updated copy and tone.







summary
After brainstorming and sketching, our group honed in on the main features we wanted for the app. We then made low-fidelity prototypes that we tested with users to make sure that our app was going to be helpful. From these results, we made revisions for further personalization and to better clarify the intent of our app.

Reflecting


analytics
We have barely scratched the surface of what we'd like to do with our app. Mental health looks different for everyone, and theoretically, we would've liked to look into analytics to study and understand if and how our app was supporting people.

To assess usefulness, some metrics to study could include:
  • Tracking number of active users to understand user base growth

  • Tracking number of returning users to understand user retention

  • Gathering aggregate mood log data to see if there is a change in overall user mood over time

  • Using heatmap analytics to understand which support tools are receiving the most engagement





future direction
Our key takeaways from this project were that a support system and personalization for both very important aspects of caring for mental health.

Future features we would've loved to incorporate are fleshing out a "buddy system" for more of a social feature among users, including more relevant personalized design cues, and expanding into the healthcare space to connect with local doctors, therapists, and other professionals.





final thoughts
I absolutely loved working on this project with my teammates. We were intimidated to tackle suicide and mental health as a topic, and we felt like we could never have enough users—we wanted our app to be as inclusive as possible and mental health presents differently for everyone.

Nevertheless, this project was a great way for us just to get started learning how to ask more and how to ask better questions as well as how to go about creating an app that hopefully feels more like a friend.
