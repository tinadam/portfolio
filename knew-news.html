Knew News, an augmented reality news app



We made a news source aggregator app with an augmented reality (AR) component so that users can quickly look up key information and background context



Timeline: 3 weeks
Project Context: Group work for UX/UI bootcamp
My Roles: UX research, UX design, UI design
Primary Tools: Figma

Researching


background
In the current digital age, we are faced with a multitude of ways to consume the news. Often, we might encounter terms that we'd like to look up for deeper understanding. We were wondering if we could create an app that can help alleviate this burden of research.





proto
persona






user
research
plan
We wanted to understand why users spent more or less time reading the news. Specifically, we wanted to:
  • Understand how users feel about the information they read

  • Discover the different tools users utilize to read the news, and how they feel about each tool

  • See if AR would be valuable for users
We carried out five Zoom interviews and surveyed eight other users through Google Forms.





key
takeaways
Based on our affinity diagram results (quick previews shown below), we saw that users
  • Mostly browsed news digitally to stay informed

  • Appreciated succint news

  • Conducted further research to understand details and relationships

  • Felt optimistic but hesitant about the potential of AR technology





Our Google Forms responses corroborated these results. Some recurring quotes of interest included:
“I do worry that my viewpoint is becoming increasingly one-sided”
“[I] check Wikipedia or Google anything that's unfamiliar”
“[AR apps are] neat but I don't really use them. The tech has a lot of potential though I think”





user
persona






problem
statement
How might we design a news app so that our users spend less time looking up information externally?





section
summary
Users generally have to go out of their way to look up further information when they're reading the news. We were wondering if there was a way we could implement AR technology to support users. We saw that our typical user likes short and simple news articles, uses Google or Wikipedia for deeper understanding, and is lightly familiar with AR technology.

After brainstorming and sketching, our group honed in on the main features we wanted for the app. We then made low-fidelity prototypes that we tested with users to make sure that our app was going to be helpful. From these results, we made revisions for further personalization and to better clarify the intent of our app.

final
thoughts
I absolutely loved working on this project with my teammates. We were intimidated to tackle suicide and mental health as a topic, and we felt like we could never have enough users—we wanted our app to be as inclusive as possible and mental health presents differently for everyone.

Nevertheless, this project was a great way for us just to get started learning how to ask more and how to ask better questions as well as how to go about creating an app that hopefully feels more like a friend.
